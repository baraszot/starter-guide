Food and drink

If you’re the one making the food for yourself, you may want to prepare it in advance before the classes. Keep in mind you may get tired throughout the day and it makes sense to have some nutritious snacks available, suited for intense intellectual, yet very sedentary work.

Your brain runs on oxygen and glucose, so it’s not entirely unreasonable of your body to crave sugar during breaks. However, if you’ve already eaten your breakfast, you most likely already have all the sugar needed in your system. What you lack is the physical activity that would help to efficiently deliver it to the brain. A sweet snack is likely to give you a rapid boost, but it’ll come with bad consequences: a glucose spike that will eventually wear you out even more. If you absolutely can’t resist carbs during breaks, try some of Jessie “Glucose Goddess” Inchauspé’s hacks: have some fibre (veggies, nuts) before you eat something sweet and/or do some exercise after you have it.

Keep yourself well-hydrated, ideally with sugar-free drinks. And be reasonable about the amount of coffee you consume. Caffeine can make you feel less tired for some time, but it’s not a magic elixir of knowledge of complicated tech comm tools. It can, however, disrupt your sleep if taken in abundance and being even more tired for the next day of classes will get you nowhere.
